Primer on Derived Distributions


    What are derived distributions?
  • Derived distributions are sampling distributions that have a probability density function that is obtained from theoretical considerations.


$\chi^2$ Distribution


    The Basics
  • Suppose we have a k independent standard normal varibles where each variable $Z_1$ .. $Z_k$ has a ~ N(0,1), then the sampling distribution for the same varibles squared $Z_1^2$ .+. $Z_k^2$ is called a chi-sqaured distribution, with k degress of freedom (df).

  • The degrees of freedom determine the shape of the curve and as a result, how the probabilities and quantiles are obtained.

  • The $\chi^2$ is denoted with this degrees of freedom in its subscript $\chi^2_k$

  • The shape of the $\chi^2$ distribution is determined solely by its degrees of freedom.

  • $\chi^2$ values only take on positive values.

  • The distribution is right-skewed and becomes less skewed as the degrees of freedom increases.

  • The mean is k

  • The mode is k - 2

  • The median is approximately $k(1 - \frac{2}{9k})^3$

  • The mean, median and mode are close to one another with then values of k is large.

  • The variance of the distribution is 2k

  • The standard deviation is $\sqrt{2k}$

  • The quantiles for the $\chi^2$ distribution is denoted in the subscript $\chi^2_k,p$ and corresponds to the quantile that cuts off an area or probability of the p in the right tail.

  • The quantity can be found in R command qchisq(p, k, lower.tail=FALSE)

Tail Probabilities from the 𝜒2 distribution¶

  • For a given $\chi^2$ value, what is the tail probability/area to the right or left
  • For tail probabilities to the right: $P(\chi^2_k > x)$
  • For tail probabilities to the left: $P(\chi^2_k < x)$
  • pchisq(x, k, lower.tail=FALSE)

Role of 𝜒2 distribution¶

  • Involved in estimatees of variability in a population
  • Distribution related to sums of squares
  • Example, if we have a random independent samples drawn from $N(\mu,\sigma)$ with n random variables $X_1,\ X_2,\ ..\ X_n$, then:
  • $ Z_j = \frac{X_j - \mu}{\sigma}\ \sim\ N(0,1)$ for j = 1, 2, ... n
  • Since all the samples are independent, then the $ Z_j^2\ \ X_j^2$ for for j = 1, 2, ... n
  • then we can conclude that each squared standardized value of $X_j$ has a $\chi^2$ distribution with a single dree of freedom and the sum of all the $X_j^2$ random variables has the $X_n^2$ distribution
  • $\Sigma_{j=1}^n Z_{j=1}^2$ = $\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\Sigma (X_j - \mu)^2$ ~ $\chi^2_n$
  • Expression can be rearranged so that we have the sum of squares: $$\chi^2_{n-1}\ +\ \chi^2_1$$
  • The degrees of freedom is n - 1


Student's t Distribution


    The Basics
  • Distribution that resembles the normal distribution but is a bit shorter and fatter

  • This distribution is used when the sample size is small and the population standard deviation is unknown.

  • The students t distribution is denoted as: $$T\ =\ \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\frac{S}{\sqrt{n}}}$$ where S is the sample standard deviation.

  • The shape of the Student's t Distribution has a single peak

  • The peak conincides with the mean and median, and are located at 0

  • The distribution is symmetric around the peak

  • The students t distribution has heavier tails than the normal distribution

  • Plot below illustrates that as the df increases, the t distribution begins to resemble a normal distribution.

Snedecor’s F Distribution


    The Basics on the F distribution
  • The F distribution is the ratio of two independent random variables with $\chi^2$ distributions drawn from a normal population

  • The ratio is F = $\frac{\frac{W}{df_w}}{\frac{V}{df_v}}$

  • Unlike the$\chi^2$ and t distribution, the F distribution has two degrees of freedom, expressed as $F_{df_w, df_v}$

Quantiles from F Distribution¶

  • $F_{df_w, df_v}$ where df_w is the degrees of freedom for the numerator and df_v is the degree of freedom of the denominator
  • qf(p, df1, df2, lower.tail=FALSE)
  • In R, this code will denote the quantile that cuts off the probability to the right of the p
