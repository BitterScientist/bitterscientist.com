F-Test comparing two population variances.


    The basics
  • The F distribution is a derived distribution with two degrees of freedom $F_{df_1, df_2}$

  • The F-test uses the F distribution to compare two population variances.

  • Unlike the t-test that is robust as long as the sample size is large enough, the F test needs to be normally distributed.

    • Assumptions:
    • Two random samples from respective populations

    • The sampels are independent of one another

    • The two populations are normally distributed with $N(\mu_1, \sigma_1)$ and $N(\mu_2, \sigma_2)$


  • Hypothesis test:
    $$\begin{aligned} H_0\colon\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2=1\quad\text{against}\quad&H_a\colon\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\ne1\\ \text{or}\quad&H_a\colon\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2>1\\ \text{or}\quad&H_a\colon\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2<1 \end{aligned}$$

    • Hypothesis test values:
    • Parameter of interest. $$\colon\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2$$

    • Point estimate. $$\colons^2/s^2$$

    • Standard error under $H_0$: $$\widehat{\mathsf{SE}}=\sqrt{\frac{s_p^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_p^2}{n_2}}=s_p\sqrt{\frac1{n_1}+\frac1{n_2}}$$

    • Test statistic. $$f=\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$$

    • Null distribution: F distribution

    • Degrees of freedom: $$df_{num}=n_1 -1$$ and $$df_{denom}=n_2 -1$$


    • Confidence intervals:

    • Calculated as $100(1- \alpha)$

    • Two-sides: $$\left(\frac{s_1^2/s_2^2}{f_{n_1-1,n_2-1,\alpha/2}}, \frac{s_1^2/s_2^2}{f_{n_1-1,n_2-1,1-\alpha/2}}\right)$$

    • Upper one-sided: $$\left(0,\frac{s_1^2/s_2^2}{f_{n_1-1,n_2-1,1-\alpha}}\right)$$

    • Lower one-sided: $$\left(\frac{s_1^2/s_2^2}{f_{n_1-1,n_2-1,\alpha}},\infty\right)$$


  • R codevar.test

Comparing two population proportions.


    The Basics
  • Procedure for comparing two population proportion is similar to comparing two population means.

  • The following procedure will compare two population proportions $p_1 - p_2$ by comparing the differenced between the two proportions $\hat{p_1} - \hat{p_2}$.

    • Assumptions:
    • Random smamples from both populations, and each sampliing unit to be success or failure.

    • The two samples are independent of one another.

    • The two sample sizes are large enough to invoke the central limit theorem.

    • Rule of thumb, $np \geq 5$ and $n(1-p) \geq 5$.


  • Hypothesis test:
    $$\begin{aligned} H_0\colon p_1=p_2\quad\text{against}\quad&H_a\colon p_1\ne p_2\\ \text{or}\quad&H_a\colon p_1p_2 \end{aligned}$$

    • Hypothesis test values:
    • Parameter of interest. $p_1 = p_2$

    • Point estimate. $\hat{p_1} - \hat{p_2}$

    • Standard error under $H_0$: $$\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})}$$

    • Test statistic. $$z=\frac{\hat p_1-\hat p_2}{\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})}}$$

    • Null distribution: $\mathsf{N}(0,1)$ Distribution

    • Degrees of freedom: None

    • The pooled sample proportion: $$\hat p_1=\frac{x_1}{n_1},\quad \hat p_2=\frac{x_2}{n_2},\quad\hat p=\frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2}$$


    • Confidence intervals:

    • Calculated as $100(1- \alpha)$

    • Two-sides: $$\left(\hat p_1-\hat p_2\pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\hat p_1(1-\hat p_1)/n_1+\hat p_2(1-\hat p_2)/n_2}\right)$$

    • Upper one-sided: $$\left(-1,\hat p_1-\hat p_2+ z_{\alpha}\sqrt{\hat p_1(1-\hat p_1)/n_1+\hat p_2(1-\hat p_2)/n_2}\right)$$

    • Lower one-sided: $$\left(\hat p_1-\hat p_2- z_{\alpha}\sqrt{\hat p_1(1-\hat p_1)/n_1+\hat p_2(1-\hat p_2)/n_2},1\right)$$


  • R method: prop.test(x, n, alt=TYPE, conf.level=CL, correct=FALSE)
