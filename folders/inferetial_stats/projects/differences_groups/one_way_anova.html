Introduction to One-Way ANOVA.


    One-Way ANOVA:
  • Is used to determine if there are any statistically significant difference between the means of two or more independent groups.

  • One-way ANOVA compares the means of two or more independent groups in order to determine whether there is statistical evidence that the associated population means are significantly different.

  • ANOVA is short for Analysis of Variance.

  • ANOVA is also known as:
    • Between-subjects ANOVA
    • One-factor ANOVA
    • One-way Analysis of Variance

  • A one-way ANOVA is an omnibus test statistic that can only tell if you if there is a statistically significant difference but does not tell you which specific groups were significantly different.

  • If you have more than three groups, a follow-up test would need to be conducted to identify which of these groups differ from each other.

  • A one-way ANOVA is a parametric test.

    Variables used in this test are known as
  • Dependent variable that is measured in the continuous level.

  • Independent variable (grouping variable, or factor) that is divided into two or more mutually exclusive levels, or groups.

    Common Use Cases
  • Field studies

  • Experiments

  • Quasi-experiments

  • Statistical differences among the mean of two or more groups.

  • Statistical differences among the mean of two or more interventions.

  • Statistical differences among the mean of two or more change scores.

  • If the One-Way ANOVA only has two groups, then the test is very much an independent sample t-test.

    Example
    • Case Determine if the there is a difference in the salary based on the type of analyst job position.

    • Dependent Variable Salary.

    • Independent Variable The type of analyst (Data Analyst, Financial Analyst, Research Analyst, Operations Research Analyst, Market Research Analyst).

    • Null Hypothesis \(H_{0} \): There is no difference in the mean salary between the analyst positions.

    • Alternative Hypothesis \(H_{A} \): The is a difference in the mean salary between the analyst positions.


    The three common case uses for One-Way ANOVA.
  1. Determine if there are differences between three or more independent groups.

  2. Determine if there are differences between three or more conditions/treatments with no pre-test measurement taken.

  3. Determine if there is differences in change scores.

    Balanced vs Unbalanced Designs.
  1. If each group of the independent variable has the same number of instances, then this is a balanced design and is idea.

  2. If each group of the independent variable does not have the same number of instances, then this is a unbalanced design.

  3. The greater the unbalance the greater the negative effect of any violation of assumptions on the validity of the test.


Discuss the Assumptions for a One-Way ANOVA.


    Assumptions

    In order to run this test, these assumptions must be considered. Failure to consider these assumptions can result in misleading and inaccurate conclusions.


    1. You have one dependent variable that is measured on the continuous scale (ratio or interval).

    2. You have one independent variable that consists of two or more categorical, independent groups. If you have two independent variables, then you should consider a two-way ANOVA.

    3. You have independent of observations, which means there is no relationship between the instances/observations in each group of the independent variable or between the groups. In other words, an instance cannot be present in more than one group.

    4. There should be no significant outliers in the groups of your independent variable in terms of the dependent variable.

    5. Your dependent variable should be approximately normally distributed for each group of the independent variable.

    6. You should have homogeneity of variance of the dependent variable.


Mathematics of One-Way ANOVA.


    Description
  • A one-way layout consists of a single factor with several levels and multiple observations at each level.

  • We can calculate the mean of the observation within each level of our factor (category)

  • The residuals will tell us about the variation in each level.

  • We can then look at the average of the mean of each level to obtain a grant mean.

  • We also look at the deviation of the mean for each level from the grand mean to understand the the level effects.

  • We then compare the variation within levels to the variation across all levels, which gives this method its name: analysis of variance.

    Model
  • The description can be modeled as follows:

    \[y_{ij} = m + a_{i} + e_{ij} \]
      The equation indicates
    • \(j\)th data value

    • \(i\) the level

    • The sum of the three components is the grand mean \(m\), the level effect \(a_{i}\), and the residual \(e_{ij}\).


  • The ANOVA table for the one-way case is given by:
      Factor
    • Sum of Squares: \(SS_{F} = J\Sigma(\bar{y_{i}} - \bar{y})^{2}\)
    • DoF: \(I-1\)
    • Mean Square: \(MSF = \frac{SS_{F}}{(I - 1)}\)
    • \(F_{0}\): \(\frac{MSF}{MSE}\)

      Residual
    • Sum of Squares: \(SS_{E} = \Sigma\Sigma(y_{ij} - \bar{y})^{2}\)
    • DoF: \(I(J-1)\)
    • Mean Square: \(MSE = \frac{SS_{F}}{(I(J - 1))}\)

      Coor.Total
    • Sum of Squares: \(SS_{T} = \Sigma\Sigma(y_{ij} - \bar{y})^{2}\)
    • DoF: \(IJ-1\)

  • Where

    \[\bar{y_{i}} = \frac{1}{J}\Sigma_{j=1}^{J}y_{ij} \]

  • and

    \[\bar{y_{..}} = \frac{1}{IJ}\Sigma_{i=1}^{I}\Sigma_{j=1}^{J}y_{ij} \]

  • The row "Corr. Total" row contains the corrected total sum of squares and the associated degrees of freedom (DoF)

  • The level effect must sum to zero. This is taken care of by most analysis programs automatically. By making the effect sum to zero, it applies a constraint to the model, and enables us to obtain a unique solution the the continuous linear model.

    Critical Region and testing
  • The One-way ANOVA tests to see if the observed data support the hypothesis that the level of the factor are significantly different from each other. This is done by comparing the within-level variance to the between-level variance.

  • If the observations within each level have the same variance, then we can calculate the variance within each level and pool these to obtain an estimate of the overall population variance. (This is the mean square of the residual.

  • If there is no level effect then the mean square across levels would be the overall variance/

  • If there is a level effect then the level mean square will be higher than the residual mean square.

  • \(F_{0}\): \(\frac{MSF}{MSE}\) provides this value.

  • If the \(F_{0}\): value is significant at a given level of significance, then there is a level effect present in the data, and we reject the null hypothesis.

  • The following steps are involved in the hypothesis testing process.

    1. Specify the null \(H_{0} \) and alternate hypothesis \(H_{A} \).

    2. Specify the level of significance \(\alpha \).

    3. Calculate the \(F_{0}\) test statistic.

    4. Determine the critical value using the test statistic value, the degrees of freedom value, and the F distribution table.

Review case and organize variables, and set-up hypothesis


  • Project: A tech company wants to determine if more frequent breaks that involve physical activity improve alertness among its software engineers that spend all there time in front of a computer screen. To test this, employees were organized into groups that received different levels of activity during their breaks. "Sedentary" = no physical activity during break, " Low" = some low intensity activity, "Moderate" = more intense activity (raises hearts bpm at a specified treshhold), "High" = intense physical activity during break.

  • Sample size: A total of 31 employees were recruited, and they randomly placed employees into four different groups, each varying the amount of physical activity they participated in during their breaks.

  • Independent Variable: The group the employees were randomly assigned (Sedentary, Low, Moderate, High).

  • Dependent Variable: The level of reported alertness when they returned to work on a continuous scale.

  • Goal: Determine if physical activity improve employee sense of alertness when they returned to work after a break.

  • The Null hypothesis \(H_{0}: \mu_{sedentary} = \mu_{low} = \mu_{mod} = \mu_{high} \): The mean difference between the paired values is zero.

  • The Alternative hypothesis \(H_{A}: \): at least one group population mean is different and they are not all equal.

  • The Level of Significance \(\alpha = 0.05\).

Confirm data does not violate assumptions


    Why confirm assumptions?
  • A critical part of the process is confirming that the data we want to analyze meets the requirements for this model.

  • If the data does not meet the models requirements, then it will provide misleading and inaccurate results.

  1. Study design contains a continuous dependent variable \(\checkmark \)
    • The dependent variable is the reported level of alterness on an continous scale.
    • The data meets the first assumption for continuous dependent variable.

  2. Independent variable is categorical with two ore more independent groups. \(\checkmark \)
    • In this study, our independent variable contains four different independent groups.
    • The data meets the second assumption for an independent variable that is categorical with two or more groups.

  3. Study contains independence of observations. \(\checkmark \)
    • In this study, employees were only assign to one group and not present in more than one group.
    • The data meets the assumption for independence of observations.

  4. There are no significant outliers in the groups of the independent variable in terms of the dependent variable. \(\checkmark \)
    • Method used Boxplot Method: Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Explore
    • Explore Dialogue box: Web landing to Factor list > Engagement to Dependent list > Click Plots
    • Explore: Plots Dialogue box: Check normality plots with tests > Un check stem-and-leaf in descriptive area > Click Continue > Return to Explore Dialogue box > Click OK
    • The box plot generated does not indicate that there is a data point that is 1.5 box-lengths from the edge of their box.
    • The data meets the assumption for no significant outliers in the groups of the independent variable in terms of the dependent variable.

  5. The dependent variable should be approximately normally distributed for each group of the independent variable. \(\checkmark \)
    • Method used Shapiro-Wilk Test: Steps above will produce the shapiro-wilk test in the output.
    • The Shapiro-Wilk Test test for normality.
    • If \(p-value < \alpha (0.05)\ Reject\ H_{0} \) Data is not normally distributed, Independent-sample t-test is not the best test to use for this study design. Suggest using the Mann-Whitney U test.
    • If \(p-value > \alpha (0.05)\ Accept\ H_{0} \) Data is normally distributed, Independent-sample t-test can be used for this study design.
    • The p-value for the Shapiro-Wilk test is greater than the level of significance \(0.05\). This means we can accept the null hypothesis that the data comes from a normal distribution.
    • The alertness score was normally distributed for the sedentary, low, moderate and high physical activity groups, as assessed by Shapiro-Wilk's test (p > .05).
    • If the data was not normally distributed, then a nonparametric test such as the Kruskal-Wallis H-Test would need to be considered. But if you have a balanced design with large sample size, then you may be able to proceed with the One-Way ANOVA as it is fairly robust to deviations from normality.


  6. Study should have homogeneity of variance of the dependent variable. \(\checkmark \)
    • Method used Levene's Test: Steps above will produce the shapiro-wilk test in the output.
    • The Levene's Test Test test for equal variance.
    • If \(p-value < \alpha (0.05)\ Reject\ H_{0} \) data does not have equal variances and this violated the assumption of homogeneity of variances.
    • If \(p-value > \alpha (0.05)\ Accept\ H_{0} \) data has equal variances and does not violated the assumption of homogeneity of variances.
    • There was homogeneity of variances, as assessed by Levene's test for equality of variances (p = .120). This assumption was met.

General Linear Model procedure for an effect size.


    Effect Size
  • The one-way ANOVA informs you whether a difference between the group means is real but does not indicate the magnitude of that effect.

  • There are different methods used to calculate the effect size, and each has their limitations.

  • Two methods to measure the effect size for a one-way ANOVA is the the partial eta square \(\eta^{2}\) and the omega squared \(\omega^{2}\) method.

  • The \(\omega^{2}\) method is calculated using the following formula:

    \[\hat{\omega}^{2} = \frac{SS_{b} - (df_{b})MS_{w}}{SS_{t} + MS_{w}} \]
    where \(SS_{b}\) is the sum of squares between groups
    \(df_{b}\) is the degrees of freedom between groups
    \(SS_{b}\) is the sum of the total sum of squares
    \(MS_{w}\) is the mean square for within groups.

  • These values can be found in the ANOVA ouput table
  • The partial eta square \(\eta^{2}\) is provided by the test of between subjects effects table, in the last column at the "group" row.

  • The preferred method is the omega \(\omega^{2}\)

    Procedure
  1. Analyze > General Linear Model > Univariate

  2. Univariate dialogue box: Alertness score to dependent variable list box > groups to Fixed Factor box > Click Options button

  3. Univariate: Options dialogue box: Select Estimates of effect size in display area > Click Continue

  4. Univariate dialogue box: Click OK Button will generate output.
