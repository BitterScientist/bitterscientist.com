Calculating Confidence Intervals
for Categorical Data.

Prequel to Confidence Intervals:
Categorical Data

  • Before we get into confidence interval, it's important to understand what is Categorical Data

  • Categorical Data: Data that can be divided into groups.

  • Examples include: race, sex, age groups, sports teams, educational level, income groups, preferences, heads or tails, and the list can go on.

  • How can numeric variables like age groups, and income groups be categorical?!

  • The example variables above are grouped and not discrete. For example, the 'Age Group' may data where the age of each instance is grouped into an age range, such as 1-10, 11-20 and 21-30. Each range is a group, and individuals who fall into a specific bin is counted in that bin. This makes this variable categorical.

  • Analysis of categorical data generally involves the use of data tables.

  • A common example is a two-way table, which represents categorical data by counting the number of observations that fall into each group for two variables. A unique category is in the rows, and the other category is the column, and the actual data inside the table are instances that contain a combination of these groups in each category.

  • The total for each category is called the marginal distribution.

  • Segmented bar graphs are a common graphical tool used to analyze categorical data.

  • Analysis of categorical data usualy involves proportions (how much of this group from the entire category).

  • Categorical data can be analyzed using single parameters, comparing two parameters, or investigating the potential relationship between two or more categorical variables (such as the two-way table).


Why Confidence Intervals Matter

  • Confidence intervals is a mathematical method to quantify the certainty (or uncertainty) of an estimate. This is important because you rarely ever have the entire population of data to work with, so you need to calculate the probability that your value is within an acceptable range.

  • Confidence Interval: A range computed using sample statistics to estimate an unknown population parameter with a stated level of confidence

  • Confidence intervals is a way of acknowledging uncertainty, and providing a range of potential values rather than a single point estimate. It's a way to be "safe".

  • In these cases we can use the sample data that we do have to construct a confidence interval to estimate the population parameter with a stated level of confidence.

  • At the center of a confidence interval is the sample statistic, such as a sample mean or sample proportion. AKA point estimate.

  • Sample proportion: is the parameter that is used when calculating the confidence interval for categorical data.

  • The width of the confidence interval is determined by the margin of error.

  • The margin of error is the amount that is subtracted from and added to the point estimate to construct the confidence interval.

  • This informaiton is visualized in the image below.

  • Margin of Error: Half of the width of a confidence interval; equal to the multiplier times the standard error

  • Confidence intervals are usually expressed as high percentages: 90% or 95%

  • This percentage suggest that the 90% or 95% range contains the sampling distribution of the sample.

  • Confidence intervals are similar to frequency tables, histograms, boxplots, and standard errors. These tools help you understand the potential error in your sample estimate.

Example

  • An online poll asked male and female football enthusiast whether footwork, decision making, or throwing ability was the most important quality in a great quarterback.

  • Question 1: Is throwing ability more important to women or men?

  • Question 2: What is the confidence inteval for this difference?

  • Data:
    • 169 women and 166 men were included in the survey
    • 58 women ranked throwing ability as the most important
    • 40 men ranked throwing ability as the most important

  • Python numpy will be used in the calculation

Part 1 of this example: Calculate the confidence interval for two proportions (yeah we'll begin with this first)

In [15]:
# get the proportions for each sex and assign over values to variables
male_proportion = 40/166
female_proportion = 58/169
male_pop = 166
female_pop = 169
women_throwing = 58
men_throwing = 40
z_95 = 1.96 # this is the z-score value for 95% confidence interval
In [16]:
# calculate the sd
sd = np.sqrt((male_proportion*(1 - male_proportion)/male_pop) + (female_proportion*(1 - female_proportion)/female_pop))
print(f'The standard error of the difference sd: {sd.round(4)}')

The standard error of the difference sd: 0.0494
In [17]:
# now calculate the 95% confidence interval as described above CI = p1 - p2 + z*sd
ci_95_upper = (female_proportion - male_proportion + z_95 * sd).round(4)
ci_95_lower = (female_proportion - male_proportion - z_95 * sd).round(4)
print(f'The confidence interval for the difference in these two proportions is:\n({ci_95_lower},{ci_95_upper})')

The confidence interval for the difference in these two proportions is:
(0.0055,0.199)


Conclusion

  • The confidence interval for the difference in the two proportions of women and men that rank throwing ability as the most important quarterback trait is (0.0055,0.199).
  • Because the confidence interval is very close to 0, it indicates that the difference in the proportions is not highly significant.
  • Next step: Test the null hypothesis that H0: p1 = p2 against one of the alternative hypothesis

Part 2 of this example: Hypothesis testing! Is the proportion of men and women that ranked throwing skills as the top quarterback skill significantly different?
